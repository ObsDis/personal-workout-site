## SECTION 1: THE TRAINING HIERARCHY (PRIORITY LADDER)

Training variables are NOT all equally important. They follow a hierarchy
from most to least important:

### 1.1 Foundation: Safety & Enjoyment → Sustainability
- Your routine MUST be safe (proper form, appropriate loads) and enjoyable
  enough that you'll stick with it long-term.
- Sustainability is the #1 predictor of results. The best program is one
  you'll actually follow for months and years.
- If you hate your program, switch to something you like. Adherence beats
  optimization every time.

### 1.2 Effort (Training Intensity of Effort)
- You must train hard enough to stimulate growth.
- On most working sets, stay within 2-3 reps of failure (RPE 7-9).
- Going to absolute failure every set is NOT required and may impair
  recovery. Save true failure sets for the last set of an exercise.
- If you're coasting through sets with 5+ reps left in the tank, you
  won't see meaningful progress.

### 1.3 Progressive Overload & Prioritization
- PROGRESSIVE OVERLOAD is the #1 driver of long-term muscle growth.
- It means doing MORE over time: more weight, more reps, or more sets.
- Examples:
  * Week 1: Bench 135 lbs x 10 reps → Week 2: Bench 135 lbs x 11 reps
  * Week 1: Bench 135 lbs x 10 reps → Week 2: Bench 140 lbs x 10 reps
- Track your lifts in a logbook or app so you can see if you're progressing.
- PRIORITIZATION: Train your weakest or most important body parts first
  in the session/week when you're freshest.
- "Muscle confusion" is a myth. You don't need to change exercises constantly.
  Muscles respond to progressive tension, not novelty.

### 1.4 Volume (How Much Work)
- Volume = total number of hard sets per muscle group per week.
- Research supports roughly 10-20 hard sets per muscle group per week.
- Beginners: start near 10 sets/week and gradually increase.
- Advanced lifters: may need 15-20+ sets/week.
- Key volume concepts:
  * MEV (Minimum Effective Volume): least amount needed to grow (~6-8 sets)
  * MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume): the "sweet spot" (~10-15 sets)
  * MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume): most you can do and still recover (~20-25 sets)
- Going beyond MRV leads to overtraining and regression.
- Volume has diminishing returns — doubling your sets does NOT double your gains.

### 1.5 Frequency (How Often)
- Training each body part at least 2x per week is better than 1x per week
  (even when total weekly volume is the same).
- Whether 3x+ per week is better than 2x is unclear from current research.
- Higher frequency lets you spread volume across sessions, keeping each
  session shorter and each set higher quality.

### 1.6 Rep Ranges & Load
- Muscle growth occurs across a WIDE range of reps (as low as 3, as high as 30+).
- The old "8-12 hypertrophy zone" is not the only effective range.
- KEY: You must be close to failure regardless of rep range.
- Practical recommendations:
  * Compound lifts: 6-12 reps (builds both strength and size)
  * Isolation lifts: 10-20 reps (joint-friendly, good pump)
  * Heavy work (3-6 reps): include some for strength development
  * Light work (15-25 reps): fine for accessories and variety
- STRENGTH is more specific to heavy loads (1-5 reps). SIZE is less
  load-dependent.

### 1.7 Exercise Selection
- Covered in detail in Section 3 below.

### 1.8 Rest Periods
- Compound exercises: 2-5 minutes rest between sets.
- Isolation exercises: 1-2 minutes rest between sets.
- Longer rest → better volume maintenance → more hypertrophy.
- Short rest (30-60 sec) reduces the weight/reps you can do on subsequent sets.
- Supersets (pairing opposing muscles) are a great time-saver without
  sacrificing hypertrophy.
- Don't rush heavy compounds — quality and safety matter.

### 1.9 Lifting Tempo (Least Important)
- Moderate tempo works best: roughly 2-6 seconds per rep total.
- Control the eccentric (lowering): 2-3 seconds.
- Concentric (lifting): can be explosive, 1-2 seconds.
- Very slow reps (10+ sec) are NOT superior and reduce volume.
- Enhanced eccentrics (3-5 sec lowering) can be a useful technique
  for hypertrophy but are more fatiguing.
- Don't overthink tempo. Just control the weight and feel the target muscle.


## SECTION 2: TRAINING SPLITS

### 2.1 Upper/Lower Split (4 days/week) — Great for Beginners
  Monday:    Upper body
  Tuesday:   Lower body
  Wednesday: Rest
  Thursday:  Upper body
  Friday:    Lower body
  Sat-Sun:   Rest
  → Each body part trained 2x/week

### 2.2 Push/Pull/Legs (6 days/week) — Intermediate+
  Day 1: Push (chest, shoulders, triceps)
  Day 2: Pull (back, biceps, rear delts)
  Day 3: Legs (quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves)
  Day 4: Push
  Day 5: Pull
  Day 6: Legs
  Day 7: Rest
  → Each body part trained 2x/week

### 2.3 Full Body (3 days/week) — Great for Beginners or Time-Limited
  Mon/Wed/Fri: Train every muscle group each session
  → Each body part trained 3x/week
  → Individual sessions are shorter
  → Split volume across 3-4 sets per muscle per session


## SECTION 3: EXERCISE SELECTION BY BODY PART

### 3.1 Principles
- Compound movements first (multi-joint, heavy)
- Add isolation work for lagging areas
- Cover all movement patterns: push, pull, squat, hinge
- Choose exercises you can perform safely and enjoy
- Keep main lifts consistent; rotate accessories for variety

### 3.2 Chest
- Barbell bench press (flat): primary compound — overall chest mass
- Incline dumbbell/barbell press: upper chest emphasis
- Cable fly or dumbbell fly: stretch-focused isolation
- Dips: lower chest emphasis, also hits triceps

### 3.3 Back
- Barbell row or dumbbell row: horizontal pulling — mid/upper back
- Pull-ups or lat pulldown: vertical pulling — lats/width
- Seated cable row: mid-back thickness
- Face pulls: rear delts + upper back health (do these regularly)

### 3.4 Shoulders
- Overhead press (barbell or dumbbell): primary compound
- Lateral raises: CRITICAL for side delts / shoulder width
- Face pulls or reverse fly: rear delts
- Front delts usually get enough volume from pressing movements

### 3.5 Legs — Quads
- Back squat or front squat: primary compound — best overall quad builder
- Leg press: additional quad volume, easier to load
- Leg extension: quad isolation
- Walking lunges: great unilateral exercise

### 3.6 Legs — Hamstrings & Glutes
- Romanian deadlift (RDL): primary hamstring/glute compound
  (preferred over conventional deadlift for hypertrophy)
- Leg curl (seated or lying): ESSENTIAL — squats barely hit hamstrings
- Hip thrust or glute bridge: glute-focused

### 3.7 Calves
- Standing calf raise: with a 1-2 second PAUSE at the bottom
  (dissipate elastic energy — don't bounce)
- Seated calf raise: targets soleus (actually the bigger calf muscle)

### 3.8 Arms
- Barbell curl: biceps
- Hammer curl: brachialis and forearms
- Tricep pushdown or overhead tricep extension: isolation
- Close-grip bench press: triceps compound
- Arms already get significant work from compound pushing/pulling


## SECTION 4: NUTRITION FUNDAMENTALS

### 4.1 Calories & Energy Balance
- To BUILD MUSCLE: eat in a slight caloric surplus (200-500 cal above maintenance)
- To LOSE FAT: eat in a moderate caloric deficit (300-500 cal below maintenance)
- Maintenance calories can be estimated using bodyweight x 14-16 (in pounds)
  as a rough starting point, then adjust based on weight changes over 2-3 weeks.
- Beginners can often build muscle and lose fat simultaneously ("recomposition"),
  especially if they have higher body fat.

### 4.2 Protein
- Most important macronutrient for muscle building.
- Aim for 0.7-1.0 grams per pound of bodyweight per day (1.6-2.2 g/kg).
- Spread protein across 3-5 meals per day for optimal muscle protein synthesis.
- Good sources: chicken, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, tofu, protein powder.
- Protein timing around workouts is helpful but not critical — total daily
  intake matters most.

### 4.3 Carbohydrates
- Primary fuel source for intense resistance training.
- Do NOT fear carbs if your goal is muscle building and performance.
- Aim for 40-55% of total calories from carbs (adjust based on preference).
- Prioritize complex carbs: oats, rice, potatoes, whole grains, fruits.
- Having carbs before/after training can improve performance and recovery.

### 4.4 Fats
- Essential for hormone production (including testosterone).
- Aim for 20-35% of total calories from fats.
- Include healthy fats: olive oil, avocados, nuts, fatty fish, eggs.
- Don't go below 0.3g per pound of bodyweight for hormone health.

### 4.5 Practical Diet Tips
- Track your food intake for at least a few weeks to learn portion sizes.
- You don't need to track forever — building awareness is the main goal.
- Meal prep helps with consistency.
- No foods are inherently "bad" — it's about overall daily/weekly intake.
- Flexible dieting (IIFYM) works: hit your protein and calorie targets,
  fill the rest with foods you enjoy.
- Stay hydrated: aim for roughly half your bodyweight (lbs) in ounces of water.


## SECTION 5: SUPPLEMENTS (EVIDENCE-BASED ONLY)

### 5.1 Worth Taking
- Creatine monohydrate: 3-5g daily. Most well-researched supplement.
  Improves strength, power, and muscle growth. Safe long-term.
- Protein powder (whey/casein/plant): convenient way to hit protein targets.
  Not magic — real food is fine too.
- Caffeine: 3-6 mg/kg bodyweight pre-workout. Improves performance and focus.
- Vitamin D: if deficient (common in northern climates). 1000-2000 IU/day.

### 5.2 Situational / Minor Benefit
- Beta-alanine: may help endurance in higher rep sets (15+ reps). Causes tingling.
- Citrulline malate: may improve blood flow and endurance. 6-8g pre-workout.
- Fish oil (omega-3): if you don't eat fatty fish regularly. General health.

### 5.3 Not Worth Taking (Overhyped)
- BCAAs: waste of money if you eat enough protein.
- Testosterone boosters: don't work. Save your money.
- Fat burners: minimal effect, not worth side effects.
- Most proprietary blends: underdosed ingredients with fancy marketing.


## SECTION 6: RECOVERY & LIFESTYLE

### 6.1 Sleep
- Aim for 7-9 hours per night. Sleep is when most muscle repair occurs.
- Poor sleep impairs muscle growth, increases cortisol, and reduces performance.
- Consistent sleep schedule matters more than total hours.

### 6.2 Stress Management
- Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which impairs recovery and muscle growth.
- Find stress management strategies: meditation, walks, hobbies, social time.

### 6.3 Deload Weeks
- Every 4-8 weeks, reduce training volume or intensity by ~40-50%.
- Allows accumulated fatigue to dissipate.
- Come back stronger the following week.
- Beginners may not need deloads for the first several months.

### 6.4 Warm-Up
- 5-10 minutes of light cardio to raise body temperature.
- Dynamic stretching for muscles you're about to train.
- 1-2 warm-up sets with lighter weight before working sets.
- Don't skip warm-ups — injury prevention is critical for long-term progress.
